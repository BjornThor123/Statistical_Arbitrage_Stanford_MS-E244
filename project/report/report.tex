\section{Introduction}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

This project investigates a statistical arbitrage strategy that exploits
mean-reversion in the \emph{idiosyncratic} component of equity options skew.
Volatility skew---the empirical observation that out-of-the-money (OTM) puts
trade at higher implied volatility than OTM calls---is a well-documented
feature of equity option markets. A common explanation is that investors
overpay for downside protection in single stocks due to temporary liquidity
demands or behavioral biases, rather than genuine shifts in the underlying risk
distribution.

While a stock's skew tends to comove with its sector (reflecting systematic
fear), deviations from this relationship can signal transient mispricings.
By stripping out the sector-driven component of skew via regression and
focusing on the unexplained residual---the \emph{idiosyncratic} skew---we
identify stocks whose skew is temporarily rich or cheap relative to its
historical norm. Positions are entered when this idiosyncratic component
deviates sufficiently from zero and are held until it mean-reverts.

The project proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data
sources and preprocessing pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the
full methodology, from volatility surface construction through to portfolio
simulation, covering two distinct implementations: a simple spot-return proxy
strategy and a delta-hedged risk-reversal options strategy.
Section~\ref{sec:results} reports backtest performance.
Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with observations and limitations.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Data Sources}

The strategy requires three data inputs:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Equity options data.} Daily option chains (calls and puts)
          across a range of strikes and maturities for each stock and the
          sector ETF. Fields used include: date, expiration date, option type,
          strike price, best bid, best offer, implied volatility, delta, gamma,
          vega, theta, and contract size.

    \item \textbf{Underlying equity prices.} Daily closing prices from CRSP for
          each stock in the universe. These are used to compute spot returns
          (Strategy~1) and to delta-hedge positions (Strategy~2).

    \item \textbf{Risk-free interest rates.} Zero-coupon yield curves matched
          to option maturities, used to compute forward prices and to invert
          the Black--Scholes formula where implied volatility is missing.
\end{enumerate}

The sample period runs from January 2018 to December 2022, spanning
pre-pandemic, pandemic shock, and post-pandemic regimes. The sector proxy is
the XLF Financial Sector ETF, and the stock universe consists of liquid
financial-sector equities for which option chains are available across multiple
maturities throughout the sample. Only options with at most 30 calendar days to
expiry are retained, consistent with a near-term skew signal targeting a 15-day
tenor.

\subsection{Data Ingestion and Storage}

Raw data files (per-ticker option CSV archives, CRSP equity files, and a
wide-format risk-free rate panel) are ingested into a local DuckDB database
via a custom \texttt{DataLoader} class. The loader handles zip extraction,
schema inference from accompanying metadata dictionaries, and type casting via
\texttt{TRY\_CAST} to handle mixed or malformed fields without aborting the
load.

\subsection{Feature Engineering and Enrichment}

After ingestion, an \texttt{options\_enriched} table is constructed by joining
the raw options table with equity prices and risk-free rates, and computing the
following derived fields:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Strike.} Raw strike prices are stored in tenths of cents in
          the source data; they are divided by $1{,}000$ to obtain
          dollar-denominated strikes.

    \item \textbf{Mid price.} $\text{mid} = (\text{bid} + \text{ask}) / 2$.

    \item \textbf{Time to expiry.} Calendar days $\tau_{\mathrm{days}}$ and
          annualised fraction $\tau = \tau_{\mathrm{days}} / 365$.

    \item \textbf{Forward price.} Using the cost-of-carry formula with the
          nearest-maturity risk-free rate $r$:
          \[
              F_t = S_t \, e^{r \tau}.
          \]

    \item \textbf{Log-moneyness.} The forward log-moneyness:
          \[
              k = \ln\!\left(\frac{K}{F_t}\right).
          \]
          Using the forward price as the normalising base ensures that
          at-the-money options correspond to $k = 0$ independent of the
          interest rate level.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Implied Volatility Imputation}

The dataset contains a meaningful fraction of options where implied volatility
is missing. Rather than discarding these observations, we impute missing values
by numerically inverting the Black--Scholes formula.

For a European call the Black--Scholes price is:
\begin{equation}
    C^{\mathrm{BS}}(S, K, r, \tau, \sigma)
    = S\,\Phi(d_1) - K e^{-r\tau}\,\Phi(d_2),
    \label{eq:bs_call}
\end{equation}
where
\[
    d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\tau}{\sigma\sqrt{\tau}},
    \qquad
    d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{\tau},
\]
and $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. For each option with a missing implied
volatility but a valid mid price, spot price, strike, and time to expiry, we
solve
\[
    C^{\mathrm{BS}}(S_t, K, r, \tau, \hat{\sigma}) = p_{\mathrm{mid}}
\]
for $\hat{\sigma}$ using Brent's root-finding algorithm on $[10^{-4},\, 20]$.
Options whose mid price violates the no-arbitrage intrinsic-value lower bound
are left as \texttt{NaN} and excluded downstream. Only OTM options are used
in the skew regression, consistent with market convention for smile fitting.

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The strategy pipeline consists of four sequential stages: (1) skew extraction,
(2) idiosyncratic skew isolation, (3) signal construction, and (4) portfolio
simulation. Two strategies share stages 1--3 but differ in how P\&L is
computed at stage 4.

\subsection{Stage 1: Skew Extraction}

For each stock $i$ and date $t$ we estimate the slope of the implied volatility
smile at a target maturity of $\tau^* = 15$ days. Skew is defined as the
derivative of implied volatility with respect to log-moneyness at a fixed
maturity:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Skew}_{i,t} = \left.\frac{\partial \sigma_i(k, \tau)}{\partial k}
    \right|_{\tau = \tau^*}.
    \label{eq:skew_def}
\end{equation}

We approximate this slope by fitting a quadratic polynomial to the
cross-section of OTM implied volatilities as a function of log-moneyness for
each (ticker, date, maturity) slice:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\sigma}(k) = \alpha + \beta k + \gamma k^2.
    \label{eq:smile_fit}
\end{equation}
The linear coefficient $\beta$ is our skew measure. OTM calls ($k \geq 0$)
and OTM puts ($k < 0$) are combined into one cross-section. A minimum of
three data points is required; slices failing this criterion return
\texttt{NaN}.

If the exact 15-day maturity is unavailable on a given day, $\beta$ is
linearly interpolated between the nearest available maturities above and below
$\tau^*$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\beta}(\tau^*)
    = \hat{\beta}(\tau_l)
      + \frac{\tau^* - \tau_l}{\tau_u - \tau_l}
        \bigl[\hat{\beta}(\tau_u) - \hat{\beta}(\tau_l)\bigr].
    \label{eq:interp}
\end{equation}
The result is a panel $\{\mathrm{Skew}_{i,t}\}$ pivoted to a wide
date $\times$ ticker matrix for downstream processing.

\subsection{Stage 2: Idiosyncratic Skew Isolation}

To separate stock-specific skew movements from sector-wide dynamics, we regress
each stock's skew on the sector ETF (XLF) skew using rolling OLS:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Skew}_{i,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i\,\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF},t}
    + \varepsilon_{i,t}.
    \label{eq:factor_model}
\end{equation}
Parameters $(\alpha_i, \beta_i)$ are re-estimated each day using only the
preceding 60 trading days, ensuring no look-ahead bias. The rolling OLS uses
the standard closed-form estimators:
\[
    \hat{\beta}_{i,t}
    = \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}_{60}(\mathrm{Skew}_i,\,\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF}})}
           {\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_{60}(\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF}})},
    \qquad
    \hat{\alpha}_{i,t} = \bar{S}_{i,t} - \hat{\beta}_{i,t}\,\bar{S}_{\mathrm{XLF},t}.
\]
The residual
\[
    \varepsilon_{i,t}
    = \mathrm{Skew}_{i,t} - \hat{\alpha}_{i,t}
      - \hat{\beta}_{i,t}\,\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF},t}
\]
represents idiosyncratic skew: the component unexplained by the sector factor,
interpreted as a transient mispricing of idiosyncratic tail risk.

\subsection{Stage 3: Signal Construction}

Residuals are standardised into rolling z-scores:
\begin{equation}
    z_{i,t}
    = \frac{\varepsilon_{i,t} - \mu_{\varepsilon_i,t}}{\sigma_{\varepsilon_i,t}},
    \label{eq:zscore}
\end{equation}
where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ are rolling 60-day mean and standard deviation
of $\varepsilon_{i,t}$.

\subsubsection{Simple Strategy: Hysteresis Filter}

The spot-return strategy uses a two-threshold hysteresis rule to reduce signal
churn near the entry boundary. Let $s_{i,t} \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ denote the
signal. Starting from the previous day's signal $s_{i,t-1}$:
\begin{itemize}
    \item From flat: enter long ($+1$) if $z_{i,t} > z_{\mathrm{entry}}$;
          enter short ($-1$) if $z_{i,t} < -z_{\mathrm{entry}}$.
    \item Hold long: remain long while $z_{i,t} \geq z_{\mathrm{exit}}$;
          flip to short if $z_{i,t} < -z_{\mathrm{entry}}$;
          otherwise exit to flat.
    \item Hold short: remain short while $z_{i,t} \leq -z_{\mathrm{exit}}$;
          flip to long if $z_{i,t} > z_{\mathrm{entry}}$;
          otherwise exit to flat.
\end{itemize}
Default parameters: $z_{\mathrm{entry}} = 1.0$, $z_{\mathrm{exit}} = 0.5$.
The exit threshold $z_{\mathrm{exit}} < z_{\mathrm{entry}}$ creates a hysteresis
band preventing the signal from toggling on small fluctuations near the
boundary.

The signal convention is: $s_{i,t} = +1$ (sell skew) means the stock's
idiosyncratic skew is unusually high and we expect it to revert down, which
in the spot-return model corresponds to going long the underlying.
$s_{i,t} = -1$ (buy skew) means the skew is unusually low, corresponding
to going short the underlying.

\subsubsection{Option Strategy: Hard Threshold}

The risk-reversal strategy uses a simpler hard-threshold rule without
hysteresis:
\begin{equation}
    s_{i,t} =
    \begin{cases}
        +1  & z_{i,t} > z_{\mathrm{threshold}}, \\
        -1  & z_{i,t} < -z_{\mathrm{threshold}}, \\
        0   & \text{otherwise},
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:signal_threshold}
\end{equation}
with $z_{\mathrm{threshold}} = 1.0$. Here $+1$ corresponds to a long risk
reversal (long call, short put) and $-1$ to a short risk reversal.

\subsection{Stage 4a: Spot-Return Strategy}
\label{sec:simple_strat}

The first strategy uses the daily spot return as the P\&L driver---a
simplified proxy for skew trading. Selling skew (long the underlying) profits
when the stock price rises, loosely consistent with a reduction in implied
skew. The daily position return is:
\begin{equation}
    r^{\mathrm{pos}}_{i,t}
    = s_{i,t-1} \times r^{\mathrm{spot}}_{i,t},
    \qquad
    r^{\mathrm{spot}}_{i,t} = \frac{S_{i,t} - S_{i,t-1}}{S_{i,t-1}}.
    \label{eq:spot_pnl}
\end{equation}

Capital is allocated equally across all active positions on each day, with
a per-position weight cap of $w_{\max} = 20\%$:
\begin{equation}
    w_{i,t} = \min\!\left(\frac{1}{N_t^{\mathrm{active}}},\; w_{\max}\right),
    \label{eq:weight}
\end{equation}
where $N_t^{\mathrm{active}} = \sum_i \mathbf{1}[s_{i,t-1} \neq 0]$.
The gross portfolio return is:
\begin{equation}
    R_t^{\mathrm{gross}} = \sum_i w_{i,t}\,r^{\mathrm{pos}}_{i,t}.
    \label{eq:gross_return}
\end{equation}

Transaction costs are charged in basis points whenever a position opens,
closes, or reverses:
\begin{equation}
    C_t
    = \frac{N_t^{\mathrm{trades}}}{\max(N_t^{\mathrm{active}},\,1)}
      \times \frac{c_{\mathrm{bps}}}{10{,}000},
    \label{eq:txn_cost}
\end{equation}
where $N_t^{\mathrm{trades}}$ counts signal changes on day $t$ and
$c_{\mathrm{bps}} = 5$ bp (one-way). Net returns are
$R_t^{\mathrm{net}} = R_t^{\mathrm{gross}} - C_t$.

\subsection{Stage 4b: Delta-Hedged Risk-Reversal Strategy}
\label{sec:option_strat}

The second strategy trades the underlying options directly. For each
(ticker, date) with a non-zero signal we construct a \emph{risk reversal}:
a long OTM call combined with a short OTM put (or vice versa), targeting
approximately 25-delta and 15 days to expiry.

\subsubsection{Leg Selection}

For each (ticker, date) the legs are selected as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{OTM call.} Restrict to calls with $k \geq 0$. Among these,
          find the maturity bucket closest to 15 days, then select the option
          whose delta is closest to $+0.25$.
    \item \textbf{OTM put.} Restrict to puts with $k < 0$. Apply the same
          maturity filter, then select the put whose absolute delta is closest
          to $0.25$.
\end{enumerate}

The risk-reversal value is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RR}_{i,t} = p^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t} - p^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t},
    \label{eq:rr_value}
\end{equation}
where $p$ denotes mid price. The net delta of a long risk reversal is
$\Delta^{\mathrm{net}}_{i,t} = \delta^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t} - \delta^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t} > 0$
(put deltas are negative by convention).

\subsubsection{P\&L Decomposition}

The daily P\&L decomposes into an option leg component and a delta-hedge
component.

\textbf{Option leg return} (normalised by spot price):
\begin{equation}
    r^{\mathrm{opt}}_{i,t}
    = s_{i,t-1}
      \times \frac{\mathrm{RR}_{i,t} - \mathrm{RR}_{i,t-1}}{S_{i,t-1}}.
    \label{eq:opt_ret}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Delta-hedge return} (from a short position in the underlying that
neutralises the risk reversal's delta):
\begin{equation}
    r^{\mathrm{hedge}}_{i,t}
    = -\,s_{i,t-1}
      \times \Delta^{\mathrm{net}}_{i,t-1}
      \times \frac{S_{i,t} - S_{i,t-1}}{S_{i,t-1}}.
    \label{eq:hedge_ret}
\end{equation}

The total position return is
$r^{\mathrm{pos}}_{i,t} = r^{\mathrm{opt}}_{i,t} + r^{\mathrm{hedge}}_{i,t}$.
Portfolio weighting and transaction costs follow the same scheme as
equations~\eqref{eq:weight}--\eqref{eq:txn_cost}.

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

Strategy performance is evaluated on daily net returns
$\{R_t^{\mathrm{net}}\}$ using:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Total and annualised return:}
          $\mathrm{TR} = \prod_t(1+R_t) - 1$;\quad
          $\mu = (1+\mathrm{TR})^{252/T} - 1$.
    \item \textbf{Annualised volatility:}
          $\sigma = \hat{\sigma}_{R} \times \sqrt{252}$.
    \item \textbf{Sharpe ratio:}
          $\mathrm{SR} = (\mu - r_f) / \sigma$.
    \item \textbf{Maximum drawdown:}
          $\mathrm{MDD} = \min_t \bigl(V_t / \max_{s \leq t} V_s - 1\bigr)$.
    \item \textbf{Calmar ratio:} $\mu / |\mathrm{MDD}|$.
    \item \textbf{Win rate, skewness, and excess kurtosis} of daily returns.
\end{itemize}

Both gross (pre-cost) and net (post-cost) versions are reported.
