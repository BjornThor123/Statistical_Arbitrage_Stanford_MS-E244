\section{Introduction}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

This project investigates a statistical arbitrage strategy that exploits mean-reversion in the \emph{idiosyncratic} component of equity options skew. Volatility skew is the empirical observation that out-of-the-money (OTM) puts trade at higher implied volatility than OTM calls and is a well-documented feature of equity option markets. A common explanation is that investors overpay for downside protection in single stocks due to temporary liquidity demands or behavioral biases, rather than genuine shifts in the underlying risk distribution.

While a stock's skew tends to comove with its sector (reflecting systematic fear), deviations from this relationship can signal transient mispricings. By stripping out the sector-driven component of skew via regression and focusing on the unexplained residual, i.e. the \emph{idiosyncratic} skew, we identify stocks whose skew is temporarily overpriced or underpriced relative to its historical norm. We enter positions when this idiosyncratic component deviates sufficiently from zero and hold until it mean-reverts.

The report is structured as follows. Section~\ref{sec:data} describes the data sources and preprocessing pipeline. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents the full methodology, from volatility surface construction through to portfolio simulation, covering two distinct implementations: a sector-regression approach and a complete pairwise trading setup. Section~\ref{sec:results} reports backtest performance and a sensitivity analysis over key modelling choices. Section~\ref{sec:conclusion} concludes with observations and limitations.

\section{Data}
\label{sec:data}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

\subsection{Data Sources}

The strategy requires three data inputs:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Equity options data.} Daily option chains (calls and puts) across a range of strikes and maturities for each stock and the sector ETF. Fields used include: date, expiration date, option type, strike price, best bid, best offer, implied volatility, delta, gamma, and contract size.

    \item \textbf{Underlying equity prices.} Daily closing prices from CRSP for each stock in the universe. These are used to compute returns for the delta-hedge positions.

    \item \textbf{Risk-free interest rates.} Zero-coupon yield curves matched to option maturities, used to compute forward prices and to invert the Black-Scholes formula where implied volatility is missing.
\end{enumerate}

The sample period runs from January 2018 to December 2022, spanning pre-pandemic, pandemic shock, and post-pandemic regimes. The sector proxy is the XLF Financial Sector ETF, and the stock universe consists of liquid financial-sector equities for which option chains are available across multiple maturities throughout the sample. Only options with at most 30 calendar days to expiry are retained, consistent with a near-term skew signal targeting a 15-day tenor. To properly handle the data we have included a detailed description of data ingestion and structuring in Appendix \ref{sec:appendixa}



\subsection{Implied Volatility Imputation}

The dataset contains a meaningful fraction of options where implied volatility is missing. A short overview of the missing values in the dataset can be found in Appendix \ref{sec:appendixb}. Rather than discarding these observations, we impute missing values by numerically inverting the Black-Scholes formula. 

For a European call the Black-Scholes price is:
\begin{equation}
    C^{\mathrm{BS}}(S, K, r, \tau, \sigma)
    = S\,\Phi(d_1) - K e^{-r\tau}\,\Phi(d_2),
    \label{eq:bs_call}
\end{equation}
where
\[
    d_1 = \frac{\ln(S/K) + (r + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2)\tau}{\sigma\sqrt{\tau}},
    \qquad
    d_2 = d_1 - \sigma\sqrt{\tau},
\]
and $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. For each option with a missing implied volatility but a valid mid price, spot price, strike, and time to expiry, we solve
\[
    C^{\mathrm{BS}}(S_t, K, r, \tau, \hat{\sigma}) = p_{\mathrm{mid}}
\]
for $\hat{\sigma}$ using Brent's root-finding algorithm on $[10^{-4},\, 20]$. Options whose mid price violates the no-arbitrage intrinsic-value lower bound are left as \texttt{NaN} and excluded downstream. Only OTM options are used in the skew regression, consistent with market convention for smile fitting. After imputation we also notice that options with short time to maturity sometimes exhibit very high implied volatilities (upwards of 800\%). This is because options near expiry are sensitive to gamma and hedging from market participants. This introduces noise into our model so we therefore remove all options with time to maturity less than 4 days. After doing this some values of implied volatilities are still extremely high (upwards of 800\%) we therefore clip values at the 90th percentile. We do this on a daily basis to avoid lookahead bias. Another approach would be to clip it at an absolute predetermined level.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}
% ---------------------------------------------------------------------------

The strategy pipeline consists of four sequential stages: (1) skew extraction,
(2) idiosyncratic skew isolation, (3) signal construction, and (4) portfolio
simulation via delta-hedged risk reversals. Two distinct approaches are
considered at stage~2, producing two strategies that share the same execution
framework at stage~4.

\subsection{Stage 1: Skew Extraction}
\label{sec:skew_extraction}

For each stock $i$ and date $t$ we estimate the implied volatility skew at a
target maturity of $\tau^* = 15$ calendar days. Only options with at most 30
days to expiry are retained; if the exact 15-day maturity is unavailable, the
skew is linearly interpolated between the nearest available maturities:
\begin{equation}
    \widehat{\mathrm{Skew}}_{i,t}(\tau^*)
    = \widehat{\mathrm{Skew}}_{i,t}(\tau_l)
      + \frac{\tau^* - \tau_l}{\tau_u - \tau_l}
        \bigl[\widehat{\mathrm{Skew}}_{i,t}(\tau_u)
              - \widehat{\mathrm{Skew}}_{i,t}(\tau_l)\bigr].
    \label{eq:interp}
\end{equation}

We consider three complementary skew measures.

\subsubsection{Direct Skew}

The simplest and most liquid measure uses the implied volatility differential
between the 25-delta OTM put and the 25-delta OTM call at the target maturity:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Skew}^{\mathrm{dir}}_{i,t}
    = \sigma^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t}(\Delta = -0.25)
      - \sigma^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t}(\Delta = +0.25).
    \label{eq:skew_direct}
\end{equation}
A positive value indicates OTM puts are expensive relative to OTM calls, the
standard signature of a negatively skewed risk-neutral distribution. Because
this measure is computed at the same strikes as the traded legs of the risk
reversal, it is directly consistent with the instrument used at stage~4.

\subsubsection{Polynomial Skew}

To exploit the full cross-section of strikes, we fit a quadratic polynomial to
all OTM implied volatilities (calls for $k \geq 0$, puts for $k < 0$) as a
function of log-moneyness $k = \ln(K/F_t)$:
\begin{equation}
    \hat{\sigma}(k) = \alpha + \beta k + \gamma k^2.
    \label{eq:smile_fit}
\end{equation}
We define the polynomial skew as the negated linear coefficient,
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Skew}^{\mathrm{poly}}_{i,t} = -\hat{\beta}_{i,t},
    \label{eq:skew_poly}
\end{equation}
so that a larger value again indicates that puts are expensive relative to
calls. Negation is required because a steeper downward slope in $\sigma(k)$
(puts pricier on the left) yields a negative $\hat\beta$ by convention.
A minimum of three data points is required per slice; slices failing this
criterion return \texttt{NaN}.

\subsubsection{Endpoint Skew}

As a robust alternative that avoids reliance on interpolation or regression,
the endpoint skew compares the implied volatility at the most out-of-the-money
put available and the most out-of-the-money call available on a given day:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Skew}^{\mathrm{end}}_{i,t}
    = \sigma_{i,t}(K_{\min}) - \sigma_{i,t}(K_{\max}),
    \label{eq:skew_endpoints}
\end{equation}
where $K_{\min}$ is the lowest available strike (deepest OTM put) and
$K_{\max}$ is the highest available strike (deepest OTM call). This measure
is conceptually similar to the direct skew but requires no delta interpolation;
it captures the full tilt of the observed smile from endpoint to endpoint.

\subsection{Stage 2a: Sector-Based Idiosyncratic Skew}
\label{sec:sector_strat}

The first approach strips out sector-wide skew dynamics by regressing each
stock's skew on the XLF Financial Sector ETF skew using rolling OLS:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{Skew}_{i,t} = \alpha_i + \beta_i\,\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF},t}
    + \varepsilon_{i,t}.
    \label{eq:factor_model}
\end{equation}
Parameters $(\alpha_i, \beta_i)$ are re-estimated each day using only the
preceding 60 trading days via the standard closed-form estimators:
\[
    \hat{\beta}_{i,t}
    = \frac{\widehat{\mathrm{Cov}}_{60}(\mathrm{Skew}_i,\,\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF}})}
           {\widehat{\mathrm{Var}}_{60}(\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF}})},
    \qquad
    \hat{\alpha}_{i,t} = \bar{S}_{i,t} - \hat{\beta}_{i,t}\,\bar{S}_{\mathrm{XLF},t}.
\]
The residual
\[
    \varepsilon_{i,t}
    = \mathrm{Skew}_{i,t} - \hat{\alpha}_{i,t}
      - \hat{\beta}_{i,t}\,\mathrm{Skew}_{\mathrm{XLF},t}
\]
represents the idiosyncratic component of skew: the part unexplained by the
sector factor, interpreted as a transient mispricing of stock-specific tail
risk. Rolling estimation ensures all parameters are computed using only
information available at the time of trading.

\subsection{Stage 2b: Complete Pairs Trading ($n^2$ Pairs)}
\label{sec:pairs_strat}

The second approach does not rely on a single sector benchmark. Instead, every
ordered pair of stocks $(i, j)$ in the universe defines its own spread:
\begin{equation}
    \varepsilon_{ij,t}
    = \mathrm{Skew}_{i,t} - \hat{\alpha}_{ij,t}
      - \hat{\beta}_{ij,t}\,\mathrm{Skew}_{j,t},
    \label{eq:pair_spread}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{\alpha}_{ij}$ and $\hat{\beta}_{ij}$ are again estimated by rolling
60-day OLS. For a universe of $n$ stocks this generates $n(n-1)$ directed
pairs, each with its own mean-reverting spread. A signal on pair $(i,j)$
translates into a position on stock $i$ hedged against stock $j$: when
$\varepsilon_{ij,t}$ is abnormally high, stock $i$'s skew is expensive
relative to its historical relationship with stock $j$, and we sell the skew
of $i$ while buying the skew of $j$.

\subsection{Stage 3: Signal Construction}
\label{sec:signal}

Regardless of whether the residual $\varepsilon_{i,t}$ comes from the sector
model (Section~\ref{sec:sector_strat}) or a stock pair
(Section~\ref{sec:pairs_strat}), it is standardised into a rolling z-score:
\begin{equation}
    z_{i,t}
    = \frac{\varepsilon_{i,t} - \mu_{\varepsilon_i,t}}{\sigma_{\varepsilon_i,t}},
    \label{eq:zscore}
\end{equation}
where $\mu_{\varepsilon_i,t}$ and $\sigma_{\varepsilon_i,t}$ are the rolling
60-day mean and standard deviation of $\varepsilon_{i,t}$. A discrete trading
signal is then generated by a hard threshold:
\begin{equation}
    s_{i,t} =
    \begin{cases}
        +1  & z_{i,t} > z_{\mathrm{entry}}, \\
        -1  & z_{i,t} < -z_{\mathrm{entry}}, \\
        0   & \text{otherwise},
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:signal_threshold}
\end{equation}
with default $z_{\mathrm{entry}} = 0.95$ (the 95th empirical percentile of the
expanding distribution of $|z_{i,t}|$, computed without look-ahead). Here
$s_{i,t} = +1$ means stock $i$'s idiosyncratic skew is abnormally high and we
expect mean-reversion back toward zero; we sell the skew of $i$. Conversely,
$s_{i,t} = -1$ means the skew is abnormally low and we buy it.

\textbf{Lookahead prevention.} To avoid any forward-looking bias, signals are
executed with a one-day lag: a signal formed at the close of day $t$ is used
to enter or exit positions at the open of day $t+1$. All rolling statistics
(OLS coefficients, z-score moments) are computed using only past observations
strictly available at the time the signal is generated.

\subsection{Stage 4: Delta-Hedged Risk-Reversal Strategy}
\label{sec:option_strat}

Both strategies execute via the same instrument: a delta-hedged \emph{risk
reversal} on the relevant stock. For each (ticker, date) with a non-zero
lagged signal, we construct a long OTM call combined with a short OTM put
(or vice versa for $s = -1$), targeting approximately $\Delta = 0.25$ and a
15-day maturity.

\subsubsection{Leg Selection}

For each (ticker, date) the legs are selected as follows:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{OTM call.} Restrict to calls with $k \geq 0$. Among these,
          find the maturity bucket closest to 15 days, then select the option
          whose delta is closest to $+0.25$.
    \item \textbf{OTM put.} Restrict to puts with $k < 0$. Apply the same
          maturity filter, then select the put whose absolute delta is closest
          to $0.25$.
\end{enumerate}

The risk-reversal value is:
\begin{equation}
    \mathrm{RR}_{i,t} = p^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t} - p^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t},
    \label{eq:rr_value}
\end{equation}
where $p$ denotes mid price. The net delta of a long risk reversal is
$\Delta^{\mathrm{net}}_{i,t} = \delta^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t} + |\delta^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t}| > 0$.

\subsubsection{P\&L Decomposition}

The daily P\&L decomposes into an option leg component and a delta-hedge
component.

\textbf{Option leg return} (normalised by spot price):
\begin{equation}
    r^{\mathrm{opt}}_{i,t}
    = s_{i,t-1}
      \times \frac{\mathrm{RR}_{i,t} - \mathrm{RR}_{i,t-1}}{S_{i,t-1}}.
    \label{eq:opt_ret}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Delta-hedge return} (from a short position in the underlying that
neutralises the risk reversal's net delta at the start of each day):
\begin{equation}
    r^{\mathrm{hedge}}_{i,t}
    = -\,s_{i,t-1}
      \times \Delta^{\mathrm{net}}_{i,t-1}
      \times \frac{S_{i,t} - S_{i,t-1}}{S_{i,t-1}}.
    \label{eq:hedge_ret}
\end{equation}

The total daily position return is
$r^{\mathrm{pos}}_{i,t} = r^{\mathrm{opt}}_{i,t} + r^{\mathrm{hedge}}_{i,t}$.

\subsubsection{Portfolio Construction and Position Limits}

Capital is allocated equally across all active positions on each day. To
prevent over-concentration in any single name, a per-stock weight cap of
$w_{\max} = 20\%$ is enforced:
\begin{equation}
    w_{i,t} = \min\!\left(\frac{1}{N_t^{\mathrm{active}}},\; w_{\max}\right),
    \label{eq:weight}
\end{equation}
where $N_t^{\mathrm{active}} = \sum_i \mathbf{1}[s_{i,t-1} \neq 0]$.
The gross portfolio return is:
\begin{equation}
    R_t^{\mathrm{gross}} = \sum_i w_{i,t}\,r^{\mathrm{pos}}_{i,t}.
    \label{eq:gross_return}
\end{equation}

\subsubsection{Transaction Costs}

Two components of transaction cost are applied whenever a position opens,
closes, or reverses.

\textbf{Options bid-ask spread.} Each risk reversal consists of two option
legs. For each leg traded, the full bid-ask spread is charged as a cost:
\begin{equation}
    c^{\mathrm{opt}}_{i,t}
    = \bigl(\mathrm{ask}^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t} - \mathrm{bid}^{\mathrm{call}}_{i,t}\bigr)
    + \bigl(\mathrm{ask}^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t}  - \mathrm{bid}^{\mathrm{put}}_{i,t}\bigr),
    \label{eq:opt_spread_cost}
\end{equation}
normalised by the spot price $S_{i,t}$ to convert to a return-equivalent.

\textbf{Delta-hedge slippage.} The delta-hedge involves trading the underlying
stock. We model equity execution costs as a flat 20 bps of the hedged notional:
\begin{equation}
    c^{\mathrm{hedge}}_{i,t}
    = \Delta^{\mathrm{net}}_{i,t-1}
      \times \frac{20}{10{,}000}.
    \label{eq:hedge_cost}
\end{equation}

Net returns are obtained by subtracting the weighted sum of both cost
components from the gross return:
\begin{equation}
    R_t^{\mathrm{net}}
    = R_t^{\mathrm{gross}}
      - \sum_i w_{i,t}\,\mathbf{1}[\text{trade}_{i,t}]
        \bigl(c^{\mathrm{opt}}_{i,t} + c^{\mathrm{hedge}}_{i,t}\bigr).
    \label{eq:net_return}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Performance Metrics}

Strategy performance is evaluated on daily net returns
$\{R_t^{\mathrm{net}}\}$ using:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Total and annualised return:}
          $\mathrm{TR} = \prod_t(1+R_t) - 1$;\quad
          $\mu = (1+\mathrm{TR})^{252/T} - 1$.
    \item \textbf{Annualised volatility:}
          $\sigma = \hat{\sigma}_{R} \times \sqrt{252}$.
    \item \textbf{Sharpe ratio:}
          $\mathrm{SR} = (\mu - r_f) / \sigma$.
    \item \textbf{Maximum drawdown:}
          $\mathrm{MDD} = \min_t \bigl(V_t / \max_{s \leq t} V_s - 1\bigr)$.
    \item \textbf{Calmar ratio:} $\mu / |\mathrm{MDD}|$.
    \item \textbf{Win rate, skewness, and excess kurtosis} of daily returns.
\end{itemize}

Both gross (pre-cost) and net (post-cost) versions are reported.

\subsection{Sensitivity Analysis}
\label{sec:sensitivity}

To assess how robust the results are to modelling choices, we vary three key
parameters one at a time while holding all others at their baseline values.

\subsubsection{Delta Target ($\Delta_{\mathrm{target}}$)}

The baseline risk reversal targets the 25-delta strike on each leg. We
re-run the backtest for $\Delta_{\mathrm{target}} \in \{0.15, 0.20, 0.25, 0.30, 0.35\}$.
Lower delta targets correspond to more out-of-the-money legs with higher
vega per unit of premium and greater sensitivity to skew movements, but also
wider bid-ask spreads and sparser option availability. Higher delta targets
move closer to at-the-money, reducing skew sensitivity but improving
liquidity. We report Sharpe ratio, net return, and average transaction cost
as a function of $\Delta_{\mathrm{target}}$.

\subsubsection{Entry Threshold ($z_{\mathrm{entry}}$)}

The entry threshold determines how extreme the idiosyncratic skew signal must
be before a position is initiated. We sweep
$z_{\mathrm{entry}} \in \{0.80, 0.85, 0.90, 0.95, 0.975, 0.99\}$ (expressed
as expanding empirical percentiles). A higher threshold admits fewer but
higher-conviction trades, reducing transaction costs at the expense of lower
capital utilisation. A lower threshold increases trade frequency and
diversification but may let in noisier signals. We examine the resulting
trade-off between Sharpe ratio, turnover, and net return.

\section{Appendix}
\label{sec:appendixa}
\subsection*{Data Ingestion and Storage}
We ingest raw data files (per-ticker option CSV archives, CRSP equity files, and a wide-format risk-free rate panel) are ingested into a local DuckDB database via a custom \texttt{DataLoader} class. The loader handles zip extraction, schema inference from accompanying metadata dictionaries, and type casting via \texttt{TRY\_CAST} to handle mixed or malformed fields without aborting the load.

\subsection*{Feature Engineering and Enrichment}
After ingestion, an \texttt{options\_enriched} table is constructed by joining the raw options table with equity prices and risk-free rates, and computing the following derived fields:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Strike.} Raw strike prices are stored in tenths of cents in the source data; they are divided by $1{,}000$ to obtain dollar-denominated strikes.
    \item \textbf{Mid price.} $\text{mid} = (\text{bid} + \text{ask}) / 2$.
    \item \textbf{Time to expiry.} Calendar days $\tau_{\mathrm{days}}$ and annualized fraction $\tau = \tau_{\mathrm{days}} / 365$.
    \item \textbf{Forward price.} Using the cost-of-carry formula with the nearest-maturity risk-free rate $r$:
          \[
              F_t = S_t \, e^{r \tau}.
          \]
    \item \textbf{Log-moneyness.} The forward log-moneyness:
          \[
              k = \ln\!\left(\frac{K}{F_t}\right).
          \]
      Using the forward price as the normalising base ensures that at-the-money options correspond to $k = 0$ independent of the interest rate level.
\end{itemize}
